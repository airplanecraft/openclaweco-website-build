Meta & Microsoft Cut Workforce to Fund Massive AI Infrastructure Investments, Signaling Strategic Shift from Human to AI Capital

April 25, 2026 · News
ai capital expenditureai infrastructurenvidia gpusmeta llamacustom silicon

Meta and Microsoft simultaneously announced significant workforce adjustments, not due to financial distress, but as a strategic pivot to channel massive savings into unprecedented AI infrastructure investments. These decisions, impacting up to 23,000 positions combined, underscore a shift from human payroll to AI capital expenditure.

Meta revealed plans to cut approximately 8,000 jobs, representing 10% of its global workforce, and eliminate 6,000 open positions, effective May 20. An internal memo from Chief People Officer Janelle Gale stated these layoffs are “part of our continued effort to run the company more efficiently and to allow us to offset the other investments we’re making.” These investments are substantial: Meta projects capital expenditure for 2026 to be between $115 billion and $135 billion, nearly double the $72 billion spent in 2025. This colossal sum is almost entirely dedicated to data centers, Nvidia GPUs, custom silicon, and the infrastructure supporting its Llama model ecosystem and the recently established Meta Superintelligence Labs. Despite reporting record full-year 2025 revenue of $201 billion and $22.8 billion in net income for Q4 2025 alone, Meta is choosing to prioritize machine investments over human workforce costs.

Microsoft's approach is more subtle but structurally identical. The company launched its first voluntary retirement program in 51 years, offering buyouts to up to 8,750 U.S. employees under a “Rule of 70” formula, where an employee’s age plus years of service must equal 70 or more. This program disproportionately targets older workers in their fifties and sixties who were instrumental in building the pre-AI Microsoft, while sales incentive plan employees are excluded. Full details are expected on May 7. CEO Satya Nadella had warned in October 2025 that 2026 would be “messy” as the industry transitions from AI demonstrations to deep AI integration. Microsoft reported robust financial results, with second-quarter fiscal 2026 revenue at $81.3 billion, a 17% year-over-year increase.

The parallel actions by Meta and Microsoft, affecting a combined 23,000 roles, clearly indicate that these workforce reductions are not driven by financial hardship. Instead, they represent a deliberate redirection of resources: substituting human payroll for massive AI capital expenditure, a pattern predicted to impact up to 96,000 tech workers by 2026. This trend highlights the intensifying strategic imperative to heavily invest in AI infrastructure across the tech sector.
